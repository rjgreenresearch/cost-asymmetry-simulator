Key Findings

  1. Structural CAR inversion: FPV drone ($500) vs PAC-3 MSE ($3.7M) yields a 0.000135 CAR — adversaries force a 7,400:1 cost exchange. This is fiscal attrition regardless of tactical outcome.
  2. All high-end interceptors deplete before day 90 under two-war stochastic demand. No system achieves the two-war standard at current inventories.
  3. Directed energy is the structural solution: Iron Beam at $2/shot versus $3.7M PAC-3 yields a 1.85M:1 CAR in the US favour — permanently inverting the cost curve for drones and rockets.
  4. Optimal marginal dollar goes to FPV drones and LUCAS for coverage-days per dollar, but THAAD production surge is the highest-priority irreplaceable scarcity gap.
  5. Quality discipline is non-negotiable at mass production scale: a 25% dud rate (Russian Geran-2 observed rate) raises effective cost per strike by 33% and erodes deterrence value proportionally.

Methodology Notes

Cost Asymmetry Ratio

CAR = (adversary unit cost) / (US defensive response cost). Quality-adjusted CAR = (adversary effective cost) / (US effective cost), where effective cost = unit cost / (1 − dud rate). Sources: CRS, CSIS Missile Defense Project, SAR FY2025/2026, CENTCOM, DoD budget docs.

Monte Carlo Demand Model

Daily demand sampled from Negative Binomial(r, p) where r and p are calibrated from mean and coefficient of variation (CV). NB accommodates overdispersion (combat demand is bursty relative to a Poisson process). Adapted from Dyna-METRIC (Sherbrooke 1968; RAND R-1872-AF). Inter-theater correlation applied via proportional mu adjustment.

Portfolio Optimisation

Greedy marginal-value algorithm; not guaranteed globally optimal but tractable and interpretable. Each ${{ portfolio.step_M }}M step allocated to the system with highest marginal gain in median days-of-coverage.
